Food4U — Connecting Surplus Food to Communities in Need

Restaurants waste large amounts of edible food while communities face food insecurity. I designed a mobile platform to enable real-time food redistribution — simplifying donations and improving access.

The Problem

Food wasted. Communities hungry. No simple system to connect them.

Restaurants frequently discard surplus prepared food due to time pressure, food safety concerns, and operational complexity — while local organizations struggle to access consistent, timely donations.

Three compounding problems: Wasted food, missed opportunities to support communities, and no simple system connecting both sides at the right moment.

How Might We…

  • Motivate restaurants to want to donate their surplus food?
  • Make it easy for restaurants to donate surplus food without adding staff burden?
Product Vision

A clear purpose from the start

Since this was a from-scratch product, establishing a clear purpose was essential before any design decisions were made. It ensured every feature tied back to the mission.

Prevent food waste by rescuing surplus food and delivering it to communities in need.

A world where no food is wasted and no community goes hungry.

Turning surplus food into meals through real-time, last-minute redistribution.

Product Principles

  • Food should not be wasted when people are hungry
  • Make donations effortless for restaurants
  • Ensure trust through safety and quality standards
  • Prioritize social impact over profit
  • Focus on maximizing meals served

These principles guided key decisions around simplicity, trust, and real-time coordination throughout the design process.

Research

Understanding both sides of the exchange

Surveys (Restaurant owners & Organizations)

Conducted separate surveys for both user groups. Insights showed a stronger opportunity and clearer pain points on the restaurant side — leading us to focus the core solution on restaurant workflows first.

User Interviews

Conducted to uncover donation challenges in depth. Found three key barriers: time sensitivity (food has a narrow window), safety concerns (liability fears), and process complexity (donation feels like extra work during a busy closing rush).

Competitive & Comparative Analysis

Evaluated existing solutions including Goodr and Too Good To Go. Most solutions lacked real-time coordination, simplicity, and reliable logistics support — creating the opportunity space for Food4U.

SWOT Analysis

Conducted to evaluate strengths, weaknesses, opportunities, and risks. Identified areas for differentiation including real-time coordination, Uber Eats logistics integration, and a dead-simple posting flow.

Secondary Research

Revealed the scale of food waste (approximately 40% of food in the US is wasted), key safety concerns (Good Samaritan laws in most states), and gaps in existing redistribution processes.

Affinity Mapping

Organized all research data to uncover patterns. Key themes: time sensitivity, safety concerns, and complexity in the donation process.

User Flow

End-to-end: restaurant posts → organization claims → Uber Eats delivers

Created an end-to-end flow connecting restaurants to organizations in real time. The flow enables quick posting, instant notifications to nearby organizations, and seamless delivery coordination through Uber Eats — all without requiring staff to manage logistics.

MVP Strategy

Focus on what makes donation fast and easy

Prioritized core actions: posting surplus food, the copy-paste repost feature, allowing organizations to view nearby listings, and enabling Uber Eats delivery to complete the logistics loop. Everything else was scoped to future iterations.

Final Design

Three core features that power the platform

Real-Time Food Listing

Smart dropdown menus allow restaurant staff to post surplus food in seconds — even during the closing rush. Pre-populated fields and a copy-paste feature for repeating items reduce effort to the absolute minimum.

[Image Placeholder — Food Listing Screen]

Nearby Discovery & Feed

Organizations see available donations on a map and feed view, with real-time updates as new listings go live. Filtering by distance, food type, and quantity helps organizations claim the most relevant donations quickly.

[Image Placeholder — Nearby Listings Map / Feed]

Claim & Delivery Tracking

Real-time pickup status and notifications reassure restaurants that food will be collected — without requiring any staff coordination. Uber Eats integration handles driver dispatch and delivery tracking automatically.

Accessibility (WCAG)

Designed for a wide range of users

Applied WCAG guidelines to ensure the app is usable and accessible for all users — particularly important given the diverse staff and volunteer base who would interact with the product.

  • Color contrast ratios checked for readability in all states
  • Clear typography hierarchy — no reliance on color alone to convey meaning
  • Touch targets sized to minimum 44×44px for comfortable use
  • Simple and intuitive navigation — minimal steps, clear labels

Usability Testing

Two rounds, with meaningful iterations between them

Conducted two rounds of usability testing (R1 & R2) and iterated the design based on user feedback between each round.

Key changes after R1:

  • Made the primary CTA more prominent — users were missing the main action button
  • Surfaced the Donation Guide on the home screen — it was previously buried but users needed it to feel confident donating
  • Simplified the posting flow by reducing the number of required fields

R2 improvements:

  • Improved visibility of key actions and reduced user effort
  • Users completed the full posting flow without guidance
  • Organizations successfully found and claimed nearby listings

Reflection

Simplifying workflows is critical in time-sensitive scenarios

I learned that in high-pressure, time-constrained environments, every extra step matters enormously. What feels like a minor friction in testing becomes a complete abandonment in the real world when a restaurant worker is doing five other things at closing time.

By reducing effort and aligning with real-world operational constraints, the design better supports both operational efficiency and social impact. If I had more time, I'd invest in deeper research with organizations on the receiving end, and explore how to design for low-tech environments where not every volunteer has a smartphone.
